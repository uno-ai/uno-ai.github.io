The Mandate

the healthcare sector's
cybersecurity mandate

Section 405(d) of the Cybersecurity Act of 2015 directed HHS to develop voluntary cybersecurity guidelines for healthcare. The result: HICP - a comprehensive framework aligned with NIST that has become the industry standard.

The 405(d) Task Group, a collaboration between HHS and the Health Sector Coordinating Council (HSCC), developed the Health Industry Cybersecurity Practices (HICP) to provide healthcare organizations with actionable, practical cybersecurity guidance. Unlike broad frameworks, HICP is purpose-built for healthcare - addressing the unique threats, constraints, and regulatory environment that health systems face daily.

OCR investigators now routinely reference HICP during breach investigations. Organizations that cannot demonstrate alignment with these recognized security practices face increased scrutiny, higher penalties, and longer remediation timelines. The 2021 HITECH Act amendment made this explicit: organizations implementing "recognized security practices" receive favorable consideration during enforcement actions.

HICP is not optional in practice. While technically voluntary, it has become the de facto standard that OCR uses to evaluate whether a healthcare organization has exercised reasonable cybersecurity diligence. Failing to align with HICP is increasingly treated as a risk factor during investigations.

Email Phishing

Social engineering attacks targeting healthcare staff to steal credentials or deploy malware.

Ransomware

Encryption attacks that halt clinical operations and threaten patient safety.

Equipment Theft

Loss or theft of devices containing unencrypted PHI and clinical data.

Insider Threats

Malicious or negligent actions by employees, contractors, and privileged users.

Connected Medical Devices

Vulnerabilities in IoMT devices that can compromise patient safety and networks.

Strategic Value

why 405(d)
matters now

The 2021 HITECH Act amendment explicitly rewards organizations that implement recognized security practices. 405(d) HICP is the most direct path to demonstrating compliance and reducing enforcement exposure.

Under the amended HITECH Act, HHS must consider whether a covered entity or business associate has implemented recognized security practices for at least 12 months when making enforcement decisions. This includes determining the amount of any fines, the duration of audits, and the nature of remedies imposed.

Organizations that can demonstrate HICP alignment receive tangible benefits: reduced penalties in breach investigations, shortened audit timelines, and more favorable settlement terms. In a landscape where OCR penalties routinely exceed seven figures, the ROI of 405(d) compliance is immediate and measurable.

Beyond enforcement benefits, HICP alignment strengthens your actual security posture. The framework was developed by practitioners who understand healthcare-specific threats. Each practice directly addresses real attack vectors that have compromised health systems - from ransomware that halts clinical operations to insider threats that expose millions of patient records.

Uno makes 405(d) compliance achievable for organizations of any size. Whether you are a critical access hospital with limited IT staff or a multi-state health system with thousands of endpoints, the autonomous assessment adapts to your organizational context and provides right-sized guidance.
